I'm writing to you directly because I need your help.

You already know me, or you know my story, or you've held me at some point on the way through. CGL. CRS. CPFT ADHD Team. CPFT Alcohol Care Team at Addenbrooke's. RCE Wellbeing Hub. The teams that are reading this are the teams that kept me alive while the underlying condition went untreated for years.

I have ADHD. It was diagnosed years ago, and I was put on a small dose of stimulant medication that was never properly reviewed. As far as I knew, it was handled. So I focused on the addiction, because that's what was on fire. I forgot about the ADHD entirely — even as it kept setting fires I didn't know it was lighting.

I went through everything the system has. A&E many times. CGL. A residential rehab where, on arrival, my ADHD medication was removed because it was a stimulant. I was at Castle Craig for ninety days. *I don't know the exact figure CGL contributed, but Castle Craig was around £1,000 a day to anyone who walked in.* The dual diagnosis was in the referral. The medication review never came. I worked thirteen hours a day in therapy. I came out fully twelve-stepped, fully addiction-informed, and with no medication underneath any of it.

I survived two years before the brain underneath started calling for what it had always needed. Then I drank. Within a week I was back in trouble. I told my parents the truth. They paid for a private psychiatric review. The psychiatrist agreed to reassess and remedicate me — but only if I could turn up medically detoxed and re-engaged with addiction services. So I engineered that. A few staged collapses. I bullied my way into A&E. The alcohol liaison nurse and I actually talked. I told her: *next time we meet, I won't be your patient. I'll be your partner.*

The psychiatrist prescribed Elvanse. Within hours of the first dose, my whole life made sense. The chaos. The relapses. The shame. The rehab where they took my medication away and called it treatment. **All of it. Not failures of willpower. Not character defects. What happens when the underlying neurological condition stays unresolved while the system treats only what arrives first.**

A month later, the same consultant psychiatrist wrote to my GP and to the CPFT ADHD Team — addressed to the team that has had me on its waiting list for seven years. The letter is in my hands. I have included a redacted image of it on this page.

> ## "ADHD previously complicated with excess alcohol use to self-medicate inadequately managed ADHD symptoms. Effective ADHD treatment matters to prevent reverting back to excess alcohol use."
>
> *— Consultant psychiatrist, in writing, addressed to CPFT ADHD Team, 2025*

I have it in writing. From an independent consultant. On letterhead. Naming the team that has been waiting to see me for seven years.

That sentence is the whole reason for this site.

## What I'm proposing

I want to test, properly, whether what happened to me happens to others. Not a full pilot. A pre-pilot demonstrator — a deliberately small, surgical piece of cohort work to find the people most like me and see whether earlier ADHD treatment changes the addiction pattern.

The published evidence is already moving in this direction. A French trial, the START study, found that **61.3% of people with addiction-treatment outcomes improved at six months when their ADHD was diagnosed and treated**. This August, *The BMJ* published a study from the University of Southampton and the Karolinska Institute, covering **148,581 people with ADHD over two years**: those on medication had measurably lower rates of suicidal behaviour, substance misuse and criminality. **Stimulants showed the strongest effect.** The lead author called the failure to treat people in time *"a major ethical issue that needs to be addressed with urgency."*

CGL has already led work in this space — earlier ADHD-and-addiction trials have run in other parts of England. The picture is consistent. The question is whether the same pattern holds for the cohort sitting in your services right now.

That is the question I am asking. And I cannot answer it without you.
