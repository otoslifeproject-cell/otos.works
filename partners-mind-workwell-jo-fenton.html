Mind · Jo Fenton · charity support

Post-medication, support became something I could actually use.

Jo Fenton and Mind matter because they show what changes when ADHD is treated well enough for support to land. This is not about charities becoming clinical services. It is about making safe, human support visible inside a wider continuity picture.

02

The support role

Good support only works if the person can reach it.

Before effective ADHD treatment, support often could not land properly. After medication, the same kind of support became usable, structured and meaningful. That difference matters to the demonstrator.

The Jo Fenton point

A strong support letter matters.

Jo can speak to the difference between someone lost in the gap and someone able to engage with support. That lived, professional observation is part of the proof picture.

03

What the demonstrator could learn

Which non-clinical supports help keep people connected?

The pre-pilot can record charity or therapeutic support as a continuity touchpoint without asking Mind, Jo or any charity partner to become a clinical pathway owner.

  • A human landing place.
  • A positive support signal.
  • A safe touchpoint for review.

04

The charity-record gap

Charities should not need patient-record access to contribute.

Public Health have already flagged the wider problem: the NHS struggles with how charities can safely participate around someone’s support when they cannot simply access clinical records. OTOS does not solve that by opening records. It solves it by carrying consent-led, bounded continuity signals.

No record access

charity support does not need the clinical file

No clinical role creep

support remains support

Consent-led

signals are bounded and agreed

Human review

no automated clinical decisions
