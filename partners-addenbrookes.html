The bedside gap

You can see the crisis. You should not be left with nowhere to send it.

I had documented combined-presentation ADHD. I had addiction issues. I had repeated emergency hospital admissions from withdrawal seizures, crisis-based detox presentations and two intensive-care admissions — including after an assault and after a delirium-tremens-fuelled jump from a first-floor balcony. The system kept seeing the fires and missing the accelerant underneath them.

The Alcohol Care Team sits at one of the most important points in that pathway: the moment when somebody is in crisis, visible, frightened enough to listen and close enough to being lost again the minute they are discharged.

OTOS is not asking Alcohol Care to diagnose ADHD or carry the next service’s risk. It is asking whether that bedside moment, which I experienced first-hand, can create a continuity touchpoint: a simple record that the person was seen, the next step was offered and the handoff did not vanish into silence.

What I imagine testing

A light touchpoint. A visible next step. A receipt that it landed.

Touchpoint

A person from the 50-person cohort is seen by Alcohol Care. The agreed OTOS touchpoint is logged.

Next step

The continuity thread is updated. Other agreed partners can see the relevant status under their own remit. Nobody takes on new clinical responsibility; the system simply stops the handoff disappearing.

Receipt

The demonstrator can see whether the next step landed, stalled or disappeared. That is the proof we are trying to gather.

The practical route

One small bedside action could become the first visible thread.

The point is not to add paperwork to a ward or turn Alcohol Care into a pathway owner. The point is to test whether one tiny, agreed action at the bedside can create a visible handoff for the cohort.

That could be a one-tap acknowledgement, a QR route, or whatever Alcohol Care says is safest and least disruptive. The mechanism can be decided together. The principle is simple: if someone is seen, the next step should not disappear.

Illustrative handoff receipt screen. Final workflow would be agreed with partners.

The conversation

Not a rollout. Not paperwork. A 30-minute conversation to pressure-test the bedside route.

If this can be done without disruption, without extra clinical burden and without confusing responsibility, it could become one of the most valuable touchpoints in the whole demonstrator. If it cannot, we agree honestly and stop there.
